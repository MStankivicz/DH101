§ 5 — On Rejection

47 titles were rejected. The most common failure modes were: (1) dead combinations — grammatically valid but semantically inert, producing no friction or surprise (e.g., "Networked Ontologies: Toward a Close Reading of Platform Studies"); (2) over-density — too many polysyllabic abstractions stacked without landing on anything specific; (3) accidental coherence — titles so plausible they read as actual papers, losing the generative joke entirely. Interestingly, some rejected titles failed by being too good. A title indistinguishable from real scholarship felt less interesting than one that hovered at the edge of sense. The sweet spot was a title that sounded real for exactly three seconds before something slipped.

§ 6 — Process Documentation

How the Generator Was Built

The generator is built in vanilla JavaScript using a hand-written Tracery-style grammar system — no external libraries. Each symbol is a named array of strings. A pick() function selects a random element from any array. The top-level #title# symbol contains twelve structural templates (title patterns), each of which calls three to five other symbols when resolved. Every generation run selects one pattern at random and recursively fills its slots.

The grammar was designed to parody a specific register: the academic humanities paper title, which follows a highly predictable formula — evocative noun phrase, colon, methodological subordinate clause. The symbol vocabulary was drawn from a real survey of recent titles in journals like New Media & Society, Cultural Studies, and PMLA. Most of the adjectives (#adjective#: 20 values), nouns (#noun#: 16 values), and fields (#field#: 12 values) appear verbatim in actual published titles. This is, itself, part of the argument.

Parameters I controlled: the vocabulary in each symbol list (every word was chosen deliberately); the twelve structural templates (which patterns are even possible); the ratio of "plausible" to "absurd" vocabulary within each list. I did not control: which template gets selected, which words fill each slot, or the resulting collisions. Those are the system's contribution.

Total outputs generated: 55 pre-loaded, plus live session outputs. The pre-loaded 55 were generated by running the grammar 70 times and selecting the 55 most interesting failures and surprises. Fifteen were discarded before numbering.

§ 7 — Artist Statement

On Creativity, Curation, and the Exhaustion of Novelty

Building this generator taught me something uncomfortable: the grammar I wrote is more constrained than I thought academic writing was. Every "fake" title the system produces follows rules that real academic titles follow. The vocabulary is real. The colon is real. The subordinate clause is real. The system doesn't parody scholarship — it reveals that scholarship already has a grammar, and that grammar is tight enough for a machine to replicate in about forty lines of JavaScript.

Where was I creative in this process? Not in the individual titles — those are the system's outputs. I was creative in the vocabulary selection: choosing words that would sit at the edge of meaning without falling off. I was creative in the template design: deciding that "Beyond #concept#: #verb_gerund# #field# through #methodology#" was a pattern worth including, while others weren't. And I was creative in curation: reading fifty-five machine-generated titles and deciding which eight revealed something true about the register they were parodying.

The question the assignment poses — "what is creativity when AI generates endlessly?" — has a different answer after you've actually done the generating. Endless generation doesn't feel like freedom. It feels like noise. After about twenty titles, a specific exhaustion sets in: not boredom exactly, but the sensation of pattern-recognition overload. You stop reading each title and start seeing the template behind it. That moment — when the system becomes visible — is when curation becomes necessary and interesting. You're no longer choosing the best title. You're choosing the title that makes the system legible, that reveals something about how it works and what it can and can't do. That choice is mine. The titles are not.
