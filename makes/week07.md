# Week 7 – Networks of Knowledge & Power

## The Artifact
**Title:** Network of Debate: AI Ethics

This artifact is a network visualization that maps relationships between major people, organizations, conferences, institutions, papers, and ideas within contemporary AI ethics debates. The graph centers on *On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots*, which acts as a major hub connecting researchers, institutions, industry labs, and broader conversations about responsible AI.

The visualization uses color-coded nodes to represent different categories:
- People
- Organizations
- Papers
- Institutions
- Companies
- Conferences
- Policies and practices

The directional edges show different forms of relationships, including co-authorship, institutional influence, critiques, publication relationships, participation in conferences, and influence on AI policy and practice.

The artifact highlights how AI ethics is not just a technical discussion, but a network of power, labor, institutions, corporations, and affected communities. It also shows how academic research can influence broader industry conversations around responsible AI and accountability. I intentionally placed “Responsible AI” near the center-right of the graph to show how many different actors converge around that concept, even when their motivations differ.

## Process Notes
I created this artifact as a network visualization focused on AI ethics debates and the relationships between influential researchers, organizations, companies, and publications. I wanted the project to show that AI ethics is not isolated to one company or one paper, but instead exists as a large interconnected system shaped by power, research, funding, and public impact.

I used a dark-background network graph style because it made the color-coded relationships easier to see visually. I organized the graph around *On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots* because that paper became one of the most influential critiques of large language models and generative AI systems.

The tools I used included Canva for layout refinement and ChatGPT for brainstorming node relationships, edge categories, and structure ideas. I also used online research and course materials to determine which people and institutions were most relevant to include.

One of my biggest decisions was simplifying the graph enough to remain readable while still communicating complexity. I had to decide which entities mattered most and which relationships were important enough to visualize. I also intentionally included both academic and corporate actors because AI ethics debates are heavily shaped by tensions between research and industry interests.

## Reflection
This project changed the way I think about AI ethics because it made me realize that ethical debates are really networks of influence and power rather than isolated arguments. Before creating this visualization, I mostly thought about AI ethics as discussions around bias or misinformation. After mapping the relationships between researchers, corporations, conferences, and institutions, I started to see how interconnected these conversations really are.

One thing that stood out to me was how central certain papers and researchers become in shaping broader public conversations. *On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots* acted almost like a bridge between academic critique and mainstream concerns about generative AI. At the same time, the visualization also showed how much influence companies like Google still have over AI development and research priorities.

I also noticed that affected communities are often visually and structurally pushed to the edges of the network, even though they are the groups most impacted by AI systems. That felt important because it reflects a real-world issue in technology development: the people most affected by systems often have the least control over them.

Overall, the project helped me understand that network visualizations are powerful because they reveal relationships that are difficult to notice in normal reading or discussion. The artifact made AI ethics feel less abstract and more like an active struggle between institutions, researchers, companies, and communities over how AI should shape society.
