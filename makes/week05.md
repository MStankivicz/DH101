## Process Notes
Part 1: Voyant Tools Analysis

I uploaded the full text of The Raven into Voyant Tools and looked through a few of the main visualizations and text analysis features.

1. Word Frequency

The most common words were nevermore, raven, Lenore, door, chamber, nothing, and bird. This shows pretty clearly how much the poem depends on repetition. Nevermore stands out the most, which makes sense because it becomes more and more important as the poem goes on. It really adds to the speaker’s downward spiral and the dark, hopeless tone. Words like door, chamber, and raven also come up a lot, which points to how focused the poem is on a small number of repeated images and objects.

2. Trends

Voyant’s Trends feature showed that Lenore appears more in the earlier and middle parts of the poem, while nevermore becomes more dominant later on. To me, that suggests the poem shifts from being mainly about grief and memory to being more about obsession and despair. The trend line for raven stays fairly steady, while nevermore spikes more strongly later. That makes it seem like the raven matters less as just a bird and more as the thing delivering the repeated word that drives the speaker deeper into his thoughts.

3. Collocates / Collocations

Voyant’s collocation view showed common pairings around words like nevermore, Lenore, and raven. Some examples were quoth raven, said raven, lost Lenore, and chamber door. These pairings help show how structured and repeated the poem really is. The repetition is not just random words showing up again and again. It is tied to specific phrases, actions, and images. Quoth raven especially stands out because it makes the bird’s speech feel almost automatic or mechanical.

4. Visualization

The Cirrus word cloud made the repeated language really obvious right away. Nevermore was the biggest word by far, and raven, Lenore, and door also stood out a lot. This helped show visually that the mood and meaning of the poem are built heavily through repetition rather than through a lot of plot development or changing scenes.

## Reflection
Part 2: GPT Analysis

I then used GPT to analyze the same text in a more interpretive way.

Prompt 1:
Please give me a literary analysis of Edgar Allan Poe’s The Raven. Focus on repetition, grief, symbolism, and the speaker’s mental state.

GPT Response Summary

GPT interpreted The Raven as a poem about grief becoming more destructive through repetition. It suggested that the raven symbolizes not just death or mourning, but also the speaker’s inability to escape his own thoughts. According to the response, the word nevermore changes in meaning over the course of the poem. At first it seems strange or maybe even a little random, but later it becomes much more painful because the speaker gives it emotional weight. GPT also focused on the setting, like the midnight hour, the dark chamber, and the tapping at the door, and explained how these details create a gothic psychological atmosphere instead of just a physical setting. It treated the raven less like a literal bird and more like a reflection of the speaker’s despair. The response argued that the poem shows how language can trap someone, especially when they are grieving and looking for answers they will never get. Unlike Voyant, GPT focused much more on tone, symbolism, and the speaker’s emotional breakdown.

Comparison

Voyant and GPT read the same poem in very different ways. Voyant points out patterns that can actually be measured, like repeated words, clusters, distribution over time, and frequent pairings. Its reading is much more structural and surface level. It cannot really explain symbolism or emotional meaning by itself, but it can show patterns that a person might miss or not fully notice while reading. GPT, on the other hand, gives an interpretive response in full language. It turns repetition into symbolism and connects the poem’s formal features to grief, psychology, and the gothic mood. It can explain what the poem might be doing, but it can also sound more certain than it should. Sometimes it smooths over ambiguity and makes the reading sound more settled than it really is. Together, the two tools show that machine reading is not just one thing. One approach counts and maps patterns, while the other builds an explanation out of them.

Reflection

When machines read literature for us, we gain different kinds of insight, but we also lose something. Voyant is useful because it makes patterns visible in a very direct way. In The Raven, it clearly showed how dominant words like nevermore, raven, and Lenore are, and it helped show how those words shift across the poem. That kind of reading can be helpful because it gives evidence for patterns that might otherwise just feel intuitive. At the same time, Voyant cannot explain why those patterns matter emotionally. It does not really understand grief, dread, or obsession. It just shows where certain words appear and how often. GPT works differently because it treats meaning as something that can be explained through interpretation. Instead of counting words, it connects them into themes, symbols, and emotional development. That makes it feel closer to actual literary analysis, but it also means GPT can sometimes sound convincing even when its interpretation is debatable. It tends to turn the text into a clear argument.

So overall, Voyant and GPT imagine meaning differently. Voyant sees meaning as pattern and structure, while GPT sees meaning as interpretation and explanation. One reads from the outside by mapping the text, and the other reads from the inside by building a narrative about it. Neither replaces human reading, but each one changes what stands out. Comparing them made it clear to me that the method you use has a huge effect on the kind of meaning you end up finding.
