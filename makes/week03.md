## Reflection
The comic explores how automated storytelling can both help and hinder personal expression. When the student first asks AI to "write me a short story about a college athlete," the output is technically competent but emotionally distant. It lacks the small, messy details—injuries, doubts, everyday frustrations—that make a life recognizably human. The student tries to fix the text by inserting those imperfections, editing for injury, recovery, and uncertainty until the story "almost feels like me." That phrase captures an important insight: representation is not just about surface features or achievements, it is about the narrative labor of making experience legible.

This week’s work made me think about the responsibilities that come with AI-assisted creativity. AI can accelerate production and suggest arcs we might not have considered, but it cannot substitute for embodied perspective. The comic shows the student doing the crucial work of personalizing AI output—choosing which struggles to include, which moments to emphasize, and how vulnerability changes tone. Those editorial choices are ethical and aesthetic acts: they shape how readers imagine a life and what they take away from a story.

In digital humanities terms, this project raises questions about authorship and authority. Who owns a narrative when part of it is generated by a model and part of it by a person refining that output? The answer matters for pedagogy and for practice: encouraging students to treat AI as a collaborator, not a crutch, foregrounds reflection and critical editing. Ultimately, the comic argues for a hybrid approach where AI helps draft, but human attention—compassionate, particular, and context-aware—makes stories meaningful.
