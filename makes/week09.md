# Week 9 – Games & Play

## The Artifact
**Title:** The Playbook — An Interactive Story

This is an interactive narrative game about football, AI, and the decisions we make under pressure. You play as Jordan Rivera, a walk-on quarterback starting his first game, using an AI system called ARIA to help make plays while learning to trust your own instincts.

**Play the game:** [The Playbook](week09-playbook.html)

The game explores what happens when human judgment and algorithmic analysis overlap. Throughout the story, you choose how much to trust ARIA's data-driven suggestions versus your own reads of the defense. Each ending reflects a different philosophy: relying on data, trusting instinct, or finding a collaborative balance. The game has three main endings and multiple branching paths based on your choices.

## Process Notes
I created this interactive narrative using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. The structure is modeled on Twine-style interactive fiction, with passages, branching choices, and multiple endings. I organized the game around a pregame moment, film session, first drive, halftime team meeting, and a final fourth-quarter decision.

The tools I used were HTML5 for structure, custom CSS for the dark athletic aesthetic, and vanilla JavaScript to manage state transitions and track choices across the story. I chose football as the setting because it's a domain where both data analytics and human intuition are actively debated in real practice.

A key design decision was making the AI (ARIA) a character that acknowledges its own limitations. Rather than positioning AI as either all-knowing or useless, ARIA explicitly says it can see patterns but not context, which creates space for the quarterback's judgment. Another decision was the halftime team moment — I wanted the final choice to come from a conversation about responsibility and ownership, not just from the player alone.

## Reflection
This project made me think about what games do that other media can't. In a story, you read about a character making a choice. In a game, *you* make the choice, and that changes how you relate to the dilemma. Playing through The Playbook multiple times, I noticed I felt differently about each ending — not because the writing changed, but because I had decided which path to take.

What struck me most was how the game's central question isn't really about AI at all. It's about leadership and trust. The quarterback has to decide not just whether to use ARIA, but how to use it in a way that doesn't abdicate his own responsibility to the team. That feels relevant to a lot of human-AI collaboration, not just sports. The most interesting ending isn't the one where you trust the algorithm most, or distrust it most — it's the one where you're transparent with your team about how you're deciding.

I also realized that games make arguments in a way linear narratives don't. By letting you play through different approaches and see where they lead, the game is saying something about what leadership looks like. It's suggesting that the best path involves listening to data, listening to your team, and then owning the decision yourself. That's something I could have *told* you in an essay, but experiencing it through choice feels different.

Creating this made me appreciate interactive fiction as a form. It forces you to think about consequence and causality — every choice matters because the story branches. That rigor is good for thinking through real dilemmas like AI adoption, where the stakes are real and the outcomes depend on how we decide to use the tools.
